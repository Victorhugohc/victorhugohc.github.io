# Ohm's Law

## Overview

Ohm’s Law defines the relationship between voltage, current, and resistance in an electrical circuit. It is one of the most fundamental principles used to analyze and design electrical systems.

It establishes how these three variables interact with each other.

## Purpose

Ohm’s Law is used to:

- Calculate current, voltage, or resistance in a circuit
- Support equipment sizing and selection
- Analyze circuit behavior under different conditions
- Troubleshoot electrical systems

It provides a simple and reliable way to understand how electrical systems respond to changes.

From this relationship:

- Increasing voltage increases current (if resistance is constant)
- Increasing resistance reduces current (if voltage is constant)
- Current depends on both voltage and resistance

This relationship applies to many electrical systems, especially resistive circuits.

## Applications

Ohm’s Law is applied in:

- Electrical circuit design
- Cable and conductor sizing
- Power distribution analysis
- Control panel design
- Troubleshooting electrical faults

It is used constantly in both simple and complex systems.

## Key Considerations

- **Linear Behavior:** Ohm’s Law applies directly to resistive elements; some devices do not behave linearly
- **Temperature Effects:** Resistance can change with temperature, affecting calculations
- **AC Systems:** In AC circuits, impedance may replace resistance in analysis
- **Measurement Accuracy:** Real-world measurements may vary due to losses and tolerances
- **System Conditions:** Faults or abnormal conditions can alter expected relationships

Using Ohm’s Law without considering real conditions can lead to incorrect assumptions.

## Practical Notes

- It is commonly rearranged depending on the variable being calculated
- Useful for quick field estimations and validation of measurements
- Forms the basis for many electrical calculations, including power and voltage drop
- Often used together with equipment ratings to verify safe operation
