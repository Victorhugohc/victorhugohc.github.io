# Voltage

## Overview

Voltage is the electrical potential difference between two points. It represents the driving force that pushes electrical charge through a conductor.

It is commonly measured in volts (V) and is one of the fundamental variables in any electrical system.

## Purpose

Voltage exists to:

- Drive current through electrical circuits
- Deliver energy from a source to a load
- Enable the operation of electrical equipment
- Define system levels for design and compatibility

Without voltage, no current flows and no electrical work is performed.

## How It Works

Voltage is created by a difference in electrical potential between two points, typically generated by a source such as a power supply, battery, or generator.

When a circuit is closed:

- Voltage is applied across a load
- This potential difference pushes electrons through the conductor
- The resulting movement of electrons is current

The magnitude of the voltage determines how strongly current is driven through the circuit, depending on the resistance.

In AC systems, voltage changes polarity and magnitude over time. In DC systems, voltage remains constant.

## Applications

Voltage is present in all electrical systems, including:

- Power distribution systems (low, medium, and high voltage)
- Industrial motors and drives
- Control circuits (typically 24V DC or 120V AC)
- Instrumentation and signal systems
- Electronic devices and automation equipment

Different applications require different voltage levels depending on power needs and safety considerations.

## Key Considerations

- **Voltage Level:** Must match equipment ratings to avoid damage or malfunction
- **Insulation Requirements:** Higher voltages require better insulation and spacing
- **Safety:** Higher voltage increases risk of electric shock and arc flash
- **Voltage Drop:** Long cable runs can reduce effective voltage at the load
- **System Type:** AC and DC voltage behave differently and affect equipment selection
- **Standardization:** Industrial systems use standardized voltage levels for compatibility

Incorrect voltage selection is a common cause of equipment failure.

## Practical Notes

- Common industrial control voltages include **24V DC**, **120V AC**, and **230V AC**
- Power systems often use higher voltages to reduce current and improve efficiency
- Always verify nominal and allowable voltage ranges in equipment specifications
- Voltage measurements should be taken under load for accurate assessment
