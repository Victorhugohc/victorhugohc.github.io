# Electrical Noise

## Overview

Electrical noise refers to unwanted electrical signals that interfere with the normal operation of circuits. It can distort signals, disrupt communication, and affect the performance of electrical and electronic equipment.

Noise is especially critical in industrial systems where power and control circuits coexist.

## Purpose

Understanding electrical noise is important to:

- Ensure reliable signal transmission
- Maintain accuracy in measurement and control systems
- Prevent malfunction of sensitive equipment
- Improve overall system stability

Managing noise is essential in modern automation and instrumentation systems.

## How It Works

Electrical noise is generated by disturbances in voltage or current caused by various sources.

Common mechanisms include:

- **Electromagnetic interference (EMI):** noise induced by nearby electrical fields
- **Switching noise:** generated by devices such as drives, relays, and power supplies
- **Conducted noise:** travels through conductors and shared power lines
- **Radiated noise:** propagates through the air as electromagnetic waves

Noise can couple into circuits through:

- Inductive coupling (magnetic fields)
- Capacitive coupling (electric fields)
- Direct conduction (shared paths)

Sensitive signals, especially low-voltage analog or communication signals, are more vulnerable.

## Applications

Electrical noise considerations are critical in:

- Instrumentation and measurement systems
- PLC and control systems
- Communication networks and fieldbus systems
- Variable frequency drives (VFDs)
- Sensor and signal wiring

Systems that combine high power and low-level signals are particularly susceptible.

## Key Considerations

- **Cable Routing:** Separation between power and signal cables reduces interference
- **Shielding:** Shielded cables help block external noise
- **Grounding:** Proper grounding improves noise immunity
- **Filtering:** Filters can reduce high-frequency noise
- **Signal Type:** Analog signals are more sensitive than digital signals
- **Environment:** Industrial environments with switching devices generate more noise

Ignoring noise can lead to unstable readings, communication errors, and unexpected behavior.

## Practical Notes

- Keep signal and power cables physically separated whenever possible
- Use twisted pair and shielded cables for sensitive signals
- Avoid ground loops, which can introduce additional noise
- Variable frequency drives are a common source of electrical noise
- Good installation practices are often the most effective way to control noise
