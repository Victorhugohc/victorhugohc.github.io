# Risk Assessment (Standards Context)

## Overview

Risk assessment is a structured process used to identify hazards, evaluate risks, and determine the level of risk reduction required in machinery and industrial systems.

Within standards, it defines how to systematically analyze a machine to ensure that risks are reduced to an acceptable level.

## Purpose

Risk assessment is used to:

- Identify potential hazards associated with a machine  
- Evaluate the severity and likelihood of harm  
- Determine required protective measures  
- Support compliance with machinery safety standards  
- Provide a documented basis for safety decisions  

It is the foundation for defining safety requirements in a system.

## How It Works

Risk assessment follows a structured methodology defined by standards.

It typically includes:

1. **Hazard Identification**
   - Mechanical, electrical, thermal, and operational hazards  
   - Human interaction points and exposure  

2. **Risk Estimation**
   - Severity of potential injury or damage  
   - Frequency and duration of exposure  
   - Possibility of avoiding the hazard  

3. **Risk Evaluation**
   - Determination of whether the risk is acceptable  
   - Identification of required risk reduction  

4. **Risk Reduction**
   - Implementation of protective measures based on defined principles  

Standards define the criteria and methods used in each step, but not a single fixed solution.

## Applications

Risk assessment is applied in:

- Machine design and development  
- Modification of existing equipment  
- Integration of automated systems  
- Safety validation and certification processes  

It is required in most machinery-related projects to demonstrate compliance and safety.

## Key Considerations

- Risk assessment must be performed early in the design process  
- It should consider all phases: operation, maintenance, and troubleshooting  
- Human factors and foreseeable misuse must be included  
- The process must be documented and traceable  
- Risk reduction must follow defined hierarchies and principles  

## Practical Notes

- Do not assume hazards are obvious; perform a systematic evaluation  
- Reassess risks after design changes or modifications  
- Involve multidisciplinary teams when possible  
- Use consistent criteria when evaluating risks  
- Incomplete risk assessment can lead to inadequate safety measures
