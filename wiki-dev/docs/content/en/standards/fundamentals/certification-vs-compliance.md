# Certification vs Compliance

## Overview

Certification and compliance are related but different concepts used to determine whether a product or system meets a standard.

Compliance means that a product is designed and built according to a standard.  
Certification means that a recognized third party has verified that compliance through testing and evaluation.

## Purpose

Understanding the difference is critical to:

- Ensure equipment can be legally installed and operated  
- Pass inspections and approvals  
- Avoid project delays or rework  
- Select components that meet project requirements  

Both concepts are used to reduce risk, but they provide different levels of assurance.

## How It Works

### Compliance

Compliance is a manufacturer’s declaration that a product meets a specific standard.

It is typically based on:

- Internal design practices  
- Engineering validation  
- Manufacturer testing  

Compliance does not always require external verification.

### Certification

Certification involves an independent organization that:

- Tests the product against a standard  
- Verifies construction and performance  
- Audits manufacturing processes (in some cases)  
- Authorizes the use of a certification mark  

Examples include listing, labeling, or marking by recognized bodies.

Certification provides documented proof that the product meets the standard.

## Applications

### Compliance is commonly used in:

- Internal engineering specifications  
- Non-regulated environments  
- Early design stages  
- Custom-built systems without mandatory certification  

### Certification is typically required in:

- Control panels and industrial equipment  
- Electrical protection devices  
- Equipment subject to inspection or authority approval  
- Installations governed by local regulations  

## Key Considerations

- Certification is often required by authorities, not just recommended  
- A compliant product may still be rejected if not certified  
- Certification scope matters (component vs complete system)  
- Different regions require different certification bodies  
- Documentation and markings must match the standard used  

## Practical Notes

- Do not assume compliance is sufficient for installation approval  
- Verify certification requirements early in the project  
- Look for official certification marks on equipment  
- For custom assemblies (e.g., panels), certification may apply to the full system, not only components  
- Missing certification is a common cause of failed inspections
