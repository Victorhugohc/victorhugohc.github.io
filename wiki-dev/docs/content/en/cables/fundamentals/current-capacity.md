# Current Capacity (Ampacity)

## Overview

Current capacity, commonly referred to as ampacity, is the maximum amount of electrical current a conductor can carry continuously without exceeding its allowable temperature.

Ampacity is one of the most critical parameters in cable design and selection, as it directly affects safety, performance, and lifespan of the cable.

---

## Purpose

The purpose of defining current capacity is to ensure that a cable operates within safe thermal limits.

If a conductor carries more current than its ampacity:

- The temperature rises
- Insulation may degrade
- The risk of failure or fire increases

Proper ampacity selection ensures reliable and safe operation of electrical systems.

---

## How It Works

When current flows through a conductor, it generates heat due to electrical resistance.

The amount of heat generated depends on:

- Current magnitude
- Conductor resistance
- Duration of current flow

At the same time, the cable dissipates heat to its surroundings.

Ampacity is determined by the balance between:

- Heat generated inside the conductor
- Heat dissipated to the environment

If heat generation exceeds dissipation, the cable temperature increases beyond safe limits.

---

## Factors Affecting Current Capacity

### Conductor Material

- Copper has lower resistance → higher ampacity for the same size  
- Aluminum has higher resistance → lower ampacity (requires larger size)

---

### Conductor Size (Cross-Sectional Area)

- Larger conductors carry more current
- Smaller conductors heat up faster

---

### Insulation Type

Different insulation materials have different temperature ratings:

- PVC: typically up to 70–90°C  
- XLPE: typically up to 90°C or higher  

Higher temperature ratings allow higher ampacity.

---

### Ambient Temperature

- Higher ambient temperature reduces heat dissipation
- Ampacity must be reduced in hot environments

---

### Installation Method

Heat dissipation varies depending on how the cable is installed:

- Open air → better cooling → higher ampacity  
- Conduit → restricted cooling → lower ampacity  
- Cable trays with grouping → reduced cooling  

---

### Grouping of Cables

- Multiple cables close together generate combined heat
- Requires derating of ampacity

---

### Duty Cycle

- Continuous loads generate more heat
- Intermittent loads may allow higher effective current

---

## Applications

Ampacity is used in:

- Selecting cable size for motors and equipment
- Designing power distribution systems
- Ensuring compliance with electrical standards
- Evaluating thermal performance in installations

---

## Key Considerations

When evaluating current capacity:

- Always consider real installation conditions, not ideal ones
- Apply correction (derating) factors when required
- Verify insulation temperature limits
- Use applicable standards (NEC, IEC) for reference values
- Consider future load increases when sizing cables

---

## Practical Notes

- Ampacity values from tables assume specific conditions—do not use them blindly.
- Undersized cables are a common cause of overheating and failure.
- Overly conservative sizing increases cost but may improve thermal margin.
- Grouping and ambient temperature are often overlooked but have significant impact.
- In industrial environments, real conditions are rarely ideal—adjust accordingly.
