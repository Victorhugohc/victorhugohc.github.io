# Cables Overview

## Overview

Electrical cables are the physical means used to transport electrical power or signals within an industrial system. They consist of one or more conductors, insulation materials, and often additional elements such as shielding or protective jackets.

In industrial systems, cables do more than connect equipment—they directly influence system performance, including energy losses, electrical noise, and overall reliability.

---

## Purpose

Cables are used to:

- Transmit electrical power from sources to loads (motors, panels, equipment)
- Carry control signals between devices (PLCs, sensors, actuators)
- Enable communication between industrial systems
- Protect conductors from environmental and mechanical conditions

Without proper cable selection, systems may experience failures, inefficiencies, or unstable operation.

---

## How It Works

A cable conducts electricity through a conductive material, typically copper or aluminum, allowing current to flow.

The insulation surrounding the conductor prevents:

- Short circuits
- Contact with other conductors or surfaces
- Current leakage

In industrial applications, additional elements may be included:

- **Shielding:** reduces electromagnetic interference
- **Outer jacket:** protects against moisture, chemicals, and mechanical damage
- **Fillers and separators:** maintain cable structure and geometry

Cable behavior depends on several factors:

- Current being carried
- Operating temperature
- Installation method (tray, conduit, open air)
- Cable length (affects voltage drop)

---

## Applications

Cables are used at nearly every level of an industrial system:

- Electrical power distribution
- Motor and variable frequency drive (VFD) supply
- Control and automation systems
- Instrumentation and measurement
- Industrial communication networks

Each application requires specific cable characteristics, making proper selection essential.

---

## Key Considerations

When working with cables in industrial systems, consider:

- **Current capacity (ampacity):** must handle load without overheating
- **Voltage drop:** long distances can affect equipment performance
- **Type of signal:** power, control, and communication require different cable designs
- **Installation environment:** temperature, humidity, chemicals, vibration
- **Electrical interference:** especially in systems with drives or sensitive signals
- **Applicable standards:** such as NEC or IEC for safety and compliance

---

## Practical Notes

- Oversizing a cable increases cost without significant benefit in many cases.
- Undersizing can lead to overheating and system failure.
- Electrical noise issues are often related to poor cable selection or installation.
- In VFD systems, cable type and shielding significantly impact performance.
