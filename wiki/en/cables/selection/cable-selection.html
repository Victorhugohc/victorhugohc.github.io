Cable Selection ​

Overview ​

Cable selection is the process of choosing the appropriate cable type, size, and construction for a specific application in an industrial system.

It combines electrical, mechanical, and environmental considerations to ensure safe operation, reliable performance, and compliance with applicable standards.

Purpose ​

The goal of cable selection is to:

  • Ensure the cable can safely carry the required load
  • Maintain proper voltage levels at the equipment
  • Protect signals from interference
  • Withstand installation and environmental conditions
  • Comply with safety and regulatory standards

Proper cable selection directly impacts system reliability and long-term performance.

How It Works ​

Cable selection integrates multiple factors into a single decision:

  • Electrical requirements (current, voltage, distance)
  • Type of application (power, control, communication, instrumentation)
  • Installation conditions (routing, environment, mechanical stress)
  • System-specific constraints (noise, flexibility, protection)

The process typically involves evaluating each factor and selecting a cable that satisfies all requirements simultaneously.

Selection Process ​

1. Define the Application ​

Identify the function of the cable:

  • Power
  • Control
  • Communication
  • Instrumentation

This determines the base cable type.

2. Determine Electrical Requirements ​

Establish key parameters:

  • Voltage level
  • Load current (FLC)
  • System type (single-phase or three-phase)

These define the electrical demands on the cable.

3. Size the Conductor ​

Select conductor size based on:

  • Current capacity (ampacity)
  • Voltage drop over distance
  • Applicable safety factors (e.g., continuous load margins)

The selected size must satisfy all conditions.

4. Evaluate Installation Conditions ​

Consider how and where the cable will be installed:

  • Conduit, tray, or open air
  • Ambient temperature
  • Grouping with other cables
  • Exposure to moisture, chemicals, or mechanical stress

Apply correction factors if needed.

5. Consider Noise and Signal Integrity ​

Determine if shielding is required:

  • High-noise environments (motors, VFDs)
  • Sensitive signals (instrumentation, communication)

Select appropriate shielding and grounding approach.

6. Verify Standards and Compliance ​

Ensure the selection meets applicable standards:

  • NEC, IEC, or local regulations
  • Equipment requirements
  • Safety margins

Example: Motor with VFD ​

Given:

  • Motor power: 30 HP
  • Voltage: 460 V
  • Full load current (FLC): 40 A

Step 1: Apply design factor

  • Continuous load adjustment (e.g., 125%):
    40 A × 1.25 = 50 A

Step 2: Select conductor size

  • Choose a cable with ampacity ≥ 50 A

Step 3: Check voltage drop

  • Evaluate based on cable length
  • Increase size if voltage drop is excessive

Step 4: Consider VFD effects

  • Use shielded cable to reduce electrical noise
  • Ensure insulation is suitable for VFD operation

Result:

  • A conductor size equivalent to 6 AWG may be appropriate depending on installation conditions and standards

Applications ​

Cable selection is used in:

  • Motor and drive installations
  • Power distribution systems
  • Control and automation systems
  • Instrumentation and measurement
  • Industrial communication networks

Key Considerations ​

When selecting cables:

  • Electrical performance: ampacity and voltage drop
  • Application type: power vs signal
  • Environment: temperature, moisture, chemicals
  • Mechanical conditions: flexibility, vibration, protection
  • Noise environment: need for shielding
  • Standards compliance: safety and regulatory requirements

Practical Notes ​

  • Cable selection is a multidisciplinary decision, not just an electrical calculation.
  • The smallest valid cable is not always the best choice—consider reliability and future expansion.
  • VFD applications often require special attention due to electrical noise and insulation stress.
  • Installation conditions frequently have a greater impact than theoretical calculations.
  • Most cable-related issues originate from incorrect selection or installation rather than cable defects.
